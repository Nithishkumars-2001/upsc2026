Syllabus Q&A — Prelims, Mains & Optional

Use the search box to jump straight to a paper, subject, or theme.

A1: Prelims consists of General Studies Paper I (100 questions) and General Studies Paper II or CSAT (80 questions), both objective type with negative marking.

A2: GS Paper I covers current events of national and international importance, Indian history and freedom movement, Indian and world geography, Indian polity and governance, economic and social development, environment and ecology, and general science.

A3: CSAT covers comprehension, interpersonal and communication skills, logical reasoning and analytical ability, decision-making and problem-solving, general mental ability, and basic numeracy.

A4: No, CSAT is only a qualifying paper requiring a minimum of 33% marks; it is not counted while determining the merit list for the Main Examination.

A5: Ancient history covers the Indus Valley Civilisation, Vedic period, Mauryan and Gupta empires, and the development of art, architecture, religion and society up to early medieval times.

A6: Medieval history covers the Delhi Sultanate, Mughal Empire, Bhakti and Sufi movements, regional kingdoms, and socio-economic changes from roughly the 8th to 18th centuries.

A7: Modern history covers the advent of Europeans, British colonial policies, socio-religious reform movements, the 1857 revolt, the national movement, and events leading to Independence and Partition.

A8: This section covers the Constitution, political system, Panchayati Raj, public policy, rights issues, and the general structure of governance mechanisms in India.

A9: This covers sustainable development, poverty, inclusion, demographics, social sector initiatives, and broad economic concepts relevant to India's development.

A10: This includes ecology, biodiversity, climate change, environmental policy, and general awareness of environmental issues without needing subject specialisation.

A11: GS Paper I covers Indian heritage and culture, history and geography of India and the world, and society, including art forms, literature, and social issues.

A12: GS Paper II covers governance, constitution, polity, social justice, and international relations, including bilateral, regional and global groupings.

A13: GS Paper III covers technology, economic development, biodiversity, environment, security, and disaster management.

A14: GS Paper IV (Ethics, Integrity and Aptitude) covers human interface of ethics, attitude, aptitude, emotional intelligence, probity in governance, and case studies.

A15: The Essay paper requires candidates to write essays on multiple topics, testing their ability to structure thoughts, provide evidence, and present a coherent, balanced argument.

A16: There are two Optional Subject papers (Paper I and Paper II), each carrying 250 marks, based on a subject chosen by the candidate from UPSC's approved list.

A17: Paper A is a qualifying Indian Language paper (or exemption for certain candidates) and Paper B is a qualifying English Language paper; both require a minimum of 25% marks but do not count toward merit.

A18: Certain candidates from northeastern states and specific categories may be exempted from the Indian Language paper as per official notification rules.

A19: Polity typically forms a significant recurring portion of GS Paper I questions each year, covering constitutional provisions, amendments, and governance structures.

A20: Geography questions commonly cover physical, Indian, and world geography, including climate, resources, and map-based questions, forming a notable share of GS Paper I.

A21: Science and Technology questions test general scientific awareness and recent developments without requiring specialised technical background.

A22: Environment and ecology-related questions have grown substantially in recent years, often forming one of the largest single topic-blocks in GS Paper I.

A23: Indian Economy syllabus covers economic planning, resource mobilisation, growth, development, employment, budgeting, and government schemes relevant to socio-economic development.

A24: Popular optional subjects include Public Administration, Sociology, Geography, Political Science and International Relations, History, Anthropology, and various literature subjects.

A25: Candidates choose one optional subject from a fixed list provided by UPSC, generally based on academic background, interest, and scoring potential.

A26: This section covers Indian art forms, literature, architecture from ancient to modern times, and cultural diversity across regions.

A27: World History covers events from the 18th century such as industrial revolution, world wars, redrawal of national boundaries, colonisation, and decolonisation.

A28: This covers Indian society's salient features, diversity, women's role, poverty, population issues, urbanisation, and social empowerment topics.

A29: Governance covers transparency, accountability, e-governance, citizens' charters, role of civil services, and government policies and interventions.

A30: This covers the historical underpinnings, features, amendments, significant provisions, and basic structure of the Indian Constitution.

A31: Social Justice covers welfare schemes for vulnerable sections, health, education, and mechanisms for protection of these sections.

A32: This covers India's relationships with neighbouring countries, bilateral and multilateral groupings, and their effect on India's interests.

A33: This covers developments in science and technology, IT, space, computers, robotics, nanotechnology, biotechnology, and intellectual property rights.

A34: This covers Indian economy planning, resource mobilisation, growth, infrastructure, and land reforms.

A35: Security covers internal security challenges, linkages of organised crime with terrorism, role of external state and non-state actors, and border security management.

A36: This covers disaster preparedness, disaster management cycles, and government mechanisms to handle natural and man-made disasters.

A37: This covers the essence, determinants, and consequences of ethics in human actions, dimensions of ethics, and ethics in private and public relationships.

A38: This covers integrity, impartiality, non-partisanship, objectivity, dedication to public service, empathy, tolerance and compassion toward the weaker sections.

A39: This covers the concept and utility of emotional intelligence and its application in administration and governance.

A40: Case studies present real-world administrative dilemmas requiring candidates to apply ethical reasoning and decision-making frameworks in their responses.

A41: Prelims tests broad factual and conceptual awareness through objective questions, while Mains demands analytical depth, structured writing, and application-based responses.

A42: Candidates are advised to build a study timetable, prioritise NCERT-level foundations, integrate current affairs, and revise systematically using standard reference sources.

A43: Yes, static portions (history, polity basics) remain largely constant, while dynamic portions (current affairs, economy, schemes) require regular updates.

A44: NCERT textbooks from Class 6 to 12 are widely recommended as they provide simple, structured foundational knowledge across history, geography, polity, and economics.

A45: Yes, Indian Art and Culture is a distinct and recurring theme tested in both Prelims and Mains GS Paper I.

A46: Current Affairs is integrated throughout the syllabus rather than being a standalone section; questions frequently link current events with static topics.

A47: These government publications are often used by aspirants to understand government schemes and policy perspectives relevant to GS papers.

A48: Yes, daily newspaper reading (such as The Hindu or Indian Express) is widely recommended to build current affairs awareness relevant to Prelims and Mains.

A49: Many aspirants start with NCERT basics, followed by standard reference books for each GS subject, integrating current affairs and answer writing practice throughout.

A50: Yes, geography-related map-based questions frequently appear in Prelims, requiring familiarity with physical and political maps.

A51: No, the syllabus expects general awareness of scientific developments rather than specialised technical depth, making it accessible to non-science graduates too.

A52: Internal security is a consistently important theme in GS III Mains, covering terrorism, cyber security, and border management issues.

A53: The Ethics paper (GS IV) carries substantial marks and can meaningfully influence overall Mains ranking, so it deserves focused preparation.

A54: Yes, since the optional subject carries 500 marks combined, strong performance in it can significantly influence the final merit position.

A55: Yes, UPSC publishes a detailed official syllabus as part of the examination notification, which should be treated as the authoritative source.

A56: Aspirants are advised to note how current events relate to static topics like polity, economy, or environment, since Mains often blends the two in a single question.

A57: There is no fixed topic list for the Essay paper; candidates choose from a set of broad themes provided in the exam, requiring general awareness and clear writing skills.

A58: Interview preparation typically involves reviewing one's DAF, current affairs, and practicing mock interviews to build confident and coherent communication skills.

A59: The core syllabus structure has remained largely stable in recent years, though aspirants should always verify against the latest official notification for any updates.

A60: This includes physical, social and economic geography of India and the world, covering topics like climate, natural resources, and population distribution.

A61: Where appropriate, well-labelled diagrams and maps can strengthen Geography-related answers in GS Paper I, though they are not compulsory.

A62: World Physical Geography covers landforms, climate zones, ocean currents and natural phenomena, tested for general conceptual understanding rather than rote memorisation.

A63: Indian Agriculture is covered under Economic Development in GS III, including cropping patterns, irrigation, storage, and agricultural marketing and policy.

A64: No advanced mathematics is required for GS papers; only basic numeracy is tested within the CSAT paper of the Preliminary Examination.
