About UPSC & this website

Search or browse through common questions about the Civil Services Examination and how this portal can support your preparation.

A1: UPSC stands for Union Public Service Commission, India's central agency responsible for recruiting officers into premier All India Services and Central Civil Services through competitive examinations.

A2: The CSE is UPSC's flagship annual examination used to recruit officers for the IAS, IPS, IFS and various other Group A and Group B central services.

A3: The examination is conducted entirely by the Union Public Service Commission, a constitutional body established under Article 315 of the Indian Constitution.

A4: This site is an independent study companion for UPSC CSE 2026 aspirants, organising the syllabus, previous papers, current affairs and preparation tips into an easy question-and-answer format.

A5: No. This is an independently run educational resource created to help aspirants; it is not affiliated with or endorsed by the Union Public Service Commission.

A6: Anyone preparing for the UPSC CSE 2026 — first-time aspirants, repeaters, working professionals, and students — can use the structured content on this site.

A7: Content is organised page-wise by topic (syllabus, previous papers, current affairs, tips, mock tests, results) and further broken down into short question-and-answer entries for quick revision.

A8: Yes, all content on this website is provided free of charge for educational purposes.

A9: Current affairs and notification-related content are reviewed periodically; however, aspirants should always cross-check dates and notifications on the official UPSC website.

A10: For the General category, candidates must be between 21 and 32 years of age as on 1 August of the examination year, with relaxations for reserved categories.

A11: General category candidates get 6 attempts, OBC candidates get 9 attempts, and SC/ST candidates have no attempt limit, subject to the upper age limit.

A12: Candidates must hold a graduate degree from a recognised university, or an equivalent qualification, to be eligible to appear.

A13: Yes, students in their final year of a qualifying degree may apply provisionally but must produce proof of passing before the Main Examination interview stage as per notification terms.

A14: The examination has three stages: Preliminary Examination (objective), Main Examination (descriptive/written), and Personality Test (interview).

A15: The Preliminary exam consists of two papers of 200 marks each (400 total), but it is only a qualifying stage and its marks are not counted for the final merit list.

A16: The Main Examination carries a total of 1750 marks across nine papers, of which seven papers count towards merit.

A17: The Personality Test (Interview) carries 275 marks, added to the Main Examination score for the final merit ranking.

A18: The number of vacancies varies each year and is announced in the official notification; historically it has ranged between roughly 700 and 1100 depending on service requirements.

A19: UPSC offers a wide list of optional subjects including Public Administration, Sociology, Geography, History, Political Science, Anthropology, and literature subjects, among others.

A20: Yes, one-third of the marks allotted to a question are deducted for each wrong answer in the Preliminary Examination's objective papers.

A21: CSAT (Civil Services Aptitude Test) is Paper II of the Preliminary Examination, a qualifying paper testing reasoning, comprehension and numerical ability, requiring a minimum of 33% to qualify.

A22: Always refer to the official UPSC website, upsc.gov.in, for authoritative notifications, syllabi, and results.

A27: The Main Examination question papers for certain subjects may be attempted in English or Hindi, or in one of the languages listed in the Eighth Schedule of the Constitution, subject to specific paper rules.

A28: Yes, a nominal examination fee applies for General/OBC/EWS male candidates; women candidates and SC/ST/PwBD candidates are typically exempted from the fee, per the official notification.

A29: The DAF is a comprehensive form filled by candidates who qualify the Preliminary exam, capturing educational, service preference and biographical details used for the Main exam and interview.

A40: Yes, topic suggestions are welcome via the Contact page and will be considered for future content updates.

A41: No educational resource can guarantee selection; success depends on the candidate's consistent preparation, aptitude, and performance across all three stages.

A42: Yes, the concise Q&A format across all pages is specifically designed to support quick last-minute revision alongside detailed study material.

A43: The Chairman of UPSC changes periodically upon appointment by the President of India; aspirants should check the official UPSC website for the current incumbent's name.

A44: The Chairman presides over Commission meetings, represents UPSC in official matters, and oversees the conduct of all examinations and recruitment processes.

A45: UPSC can have a Chairman and not more than ten other members, as prescribed under Article 316 of the Constitution, appointed by the President.

A46: A UPSC member holds office for a term of six years or until attaining the age of 65 years, whichever is earlier.

A47: UPSC members can only be removed by an order of the President following specific constitutional grounds and procedure, ensuring the Commission's independence.

A48: UPSC submits an annual report on its work to the President, which is laid before both Houses of Parliament, providing a form of accountability.

A49: No, UPSC conducts numerous other examinations too, including the Engineering Services Exam, Combined Defence Services Exam, and NDA/NA exam, among others.

A50: IAS (Indian Administrative Service) officers primarily handle civil administration and policy implementation, while IPS (Indian Police Service) officers lead police forces and maintain law and order.

A51: In the civil services context, IFS refers to the Indian Foreign Service, responsible for representing India diplomatically abroad; note this differs from the Indian Forest Service, also abbreviated IFS.

A52: All India Services comprise IAS, IPS, and IFoS (Indian Forest Service) — services common to both the Union and State governments.

A53: Central Civil Services are services under the Union Government only, such as Indian Revenue Service, Indian Foreign Service, and Indian Postal Service, among others.

A54: The exam is considered challenging due to its vast syllabus, multi-stage format, high competition-to-vacancy ratio, and the analytical depth required in the Main Examination.

A55: From notification to final result, the UPSC CSE cycle typically spans about 10 to 12 months, covering Prelims, Mains, and Interview stages.

A56: Yes, many working professionals successfully prepare for UPSC CSE through disciplined time management, though it requires significant consistent effort.

A57: The Essay paper tests a candidate's ability to organise ideas coherently, present balanced arguments, and write within a structured format on a given topic.

A58: The Main Examination includes four General Studies papers (GS I to GS IV) covering history, geography, governance, economy, science, ethics, and current issues.

A59: GS IV tests a candidate's approach to integrity, ethical dilemmas, aptitude and case studies relevant to public administration and governance.

A60: The Current Affairs page provides curated, revision-focused content; for daily breaking updates, aspirants should also consult dedicated news sources.

A61: A beginner should first read the complete syllabus, understand the exam pattern, start with NCERT-level basics, and gradually build a daily current affairs and answer-writing habit.
